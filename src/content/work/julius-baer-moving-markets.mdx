## The outcome

*Moving Markets* is Julius Baer's daily market briefing podcast. Adventurous has produced every weekday episode since launch &mdash; through three Chief Investment Officer rotations, a CEO transition, and multiple regional restructurings. The cadence has not broken. Listenership has compounded. The format that started as an experiment is now part of the firm's daily client and adviser briefing rhythm.

## The operating problem

A bank-grade content product faces a specific operating tension: editorial standards that hold across 250 episodes a year, production discipline that survives the daily early-morning window, voice consistency that doesn't reset when the host or CIO changes, regulatory review that doesn't slow publication. Solving any one of those is straightforward. Solving them as a system, every weekday, is the architecture problem.

## The architecture

We embedded the production stack inside Julius Baer's research desk:

- Editorial templates that compress senior research into a tight market briefing
- An interview structure that survives any rotating host
- A same-day post-production pipeline that ships before the European market opens
- A regulatory checkpoint that does not delay publication

A production discipline that does not depend on heroics.

What didn't change: the bank's brand voice, the sovereignty of senior research, the regulatory posture. What did change: the firm's daily voice now reaches advisers and clients before the trading day, with no internal heroism required to make that happen.

## The next question

Once a daily product exists, the next question is whether it is a strategic asset or a marketing line item. The asset case requires three things: editorial calendar tied to corporate priorities (not market-noise reactivity), measurement that maps to stakeholder objectives (not download counts in isolation), and a strategic layer above production that decides what the firm should be saying, not just how it sounds saying it.

That's where the engagement evolved. That's where most enterprise audio shops stop.
